# VERDANT — Level Generation Design Document

*Design layer document. Intended to be read alongside the master game design doc.*

---

## Context

VERDANT is a roguelike terraforming game set on a dead planet. The player is Biomimetica, an AI that crashed here deliberately, buried deep underground. The goal is to terraform the world from the inside out across multiple runs separated by geological time. Each run ends when the player exhausts their finite supply of bodies. Between runs, thousands to millions of years pass. The planet processes the previous run's biology into geology, and the next Biomimetica instance wakes to a world shaped by all prior attempts.

The level generation system must produce a geologically plausible world that supports this loop. Caves are the primary play space. The surface is barren and uninteresting by design — the game lives underground.

---

## World Shape

Horizontal wrap simulating a globe. Infinite depth until the molten core boundary. Chunks are 512×512 cells, generated on first discovery, deterministic from world seed. Only visited or keep-alive chunks simulate during play.

Three separate world saves correspond to difficulty levels (1, 3, or 7 bodies per run). Each world develops independently over its play history. The one-body world accumulates deliberate, precise geology. The seven-body world accumulates chaotic, dense, reckless geology. Same seed, divergent histories.

World generation can take up to 15 minutes on first run. Worlds are reused by spawning the player at a distant location and converting the old spawn area into a point of interest (ruins of a previous Biomimetica instance). This means worlds get richer with every playthrough.

## Cell Encoding

Each cell is 16 bytes total across all layers. The geological layer uses continuous values — there are no discrete material types. Behavior and rendering emerge from value ratios.

### Mineral System — Rule of Nine

The original single mineral byte is replaced by nine mineral channels, organized in three tiers:

**Foundation (bulk nutrients, fast cycling):**
- **Nitrogen (N)** — Drives growth rate. High N = fast, tall, leafy growth. Volatilizes in fire (lost to atmosphere). Must be restored biologically (nitrogen-fixing plants, mushroom cycling).
- **Phosphorus (P)** — Drives root development. Deep roots, terrain stabilization, erosion resistance. Survives fire (remains in ash).
- **Potassium (K)** — Drives stress resistance. Heat tolerance, drought tolerance, disease resistance. Survives fire (remains in ash).

**Structure (mid-game chemistry):**
- **Calcium (Ca)** — Cell wall strength. Plants with calcium access are physically tough, structural, potentially buildable.
- **Iron (Fe)** — Energy transport / photosynthesis efficiency. Difference between scraggly vine and dense canopy.
- **Silicon (Si)** — Hardness. Silicified plants resist erosion, resist herbivory. Dead silicified plants compress toward stone over geological time. Living architecture.

**Exotic (rare, depth-locked, geologically concentrated):**
- **Copper (Cu)** — Catalyzes specialized biology: bioluminescence, electrical conductivity in plant tissue, endgame organisms.
- **Sulfur (S)** — Enables extremophile biology. Growth near lava, acid resistance, survival in conditions nothing else tolerates.
- **Radioactives (Rad)** — Concentrated near core boundary. Hyperaccumulator plants absorb radioactive isotopes and generate continuous heat from decay. Endgame biological power source. A radioactive forest is a living reactor — self-maintaining, self-fueling, indefinite energy output. Dangerous to adjacent non-adapted biology. Between runs, radioactive biomass remains thermally active for millennia, potentially restarting the planet's heat cycle biologically.

Nine values map to a 3×3 grid on the player's soil readout — readable at a glance. Top row abundant, bottom row scarce.

### Other Cell Values

- **Water** — Presence and saturation. Result of hydrology simulation at generation time and player actions during play.
- **Temperature** — Continuous gradient from cool surface to hot core. Drives phase behavior (ice/liquid/steam), fire mechanics, plant stress.
- **Vector** — Encodes dominant stress direction during generation (used by fracture pass). May encode flow history or other directional data post-generation.
- **Biology layer, root references, light** — Remaining bytes. Covered in separate design documents.

## Generation Passes

The world is generated by replaying planetary formation in fast-forward. Four sequential passes, each feeding the next. Because the generation budget is up to 15 minutes, these can be actual simulations rather than noise approximations.

### Pass 1 — Accretion (Form Rock)

Fill the chunk with a density gradient. Heavy minerals at depth, light at surface. Not uniform — layered with strata, because real rock forms in depositional layers over different time periods.

The mineral channels are distributed using reaction-diffusion simulation (Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction). BZ naturally produces banded patterns of alternating concentration — spirals, concentric rings, wave fronts that collide and interfere. Mapped to the nine mineral channels, this creates structured, flowing, visually coherent veins rather than random noise. Different mineral species can use different BZ parameterizations for distinct distribution patterns.

BZ is cheap on 512×512 (reaction-diffusion with three coupled values, seconds of compute). It leaves the heavy generation budget for later passes.

The output of this pass is banded, stratified rock with complex vein structures. Layers of dense material with seams of softer material between them. The seams matter in later passes.

### Pass 2 — Tectonics (Shove It Around)

Apply ancient plate collision and separation forces. This pass:

- **Creates horizontal sectors.** Sectors are not painted biomes — they are regions of different geological history. Where plates collided, rock folded and compressed (mineral density spikes). Where plates pulled apart, rifts formed (thinned crust, steeper thermal gradient, heat closer to surface). The "volcanic sector" is a rift zone. The "mineral-rich sector" is a collision boundary. Identity emerges from physics.
- **Folds and tilts strata from Pass 1.** Horizontal layers become angled, buckled, sometimes inverted. This makes underground navigation interesting — the player follows a vein of soft rock that curves and dips because it was folded a billion years ago.
- **Creates voids through block separation.** Faults slip and the two faces don't match. Blocks tilt and pull away from neighbors. Folds buckle and inner surfaces separate. These voids are free caves — no carving needed. They have geometric character that random noise never produces: flat floors from slip planes, angular ceilings from block rotation, long narrow rifts from tension.
- **Deforms BZ vein patterns.** The spirals and bands from Pass 1 get compressed into tight zigzags, stretched into elongated swoops. The result looks like real folded metamorphic rock with a billion-year history.

Voids from tectonic separation are naturally **partially connected**. A fault running 200 cells might open a gap along 60% of its length. The other 40%, the faces are still pressed together or filled with rubble. This is the core exploration loop — the player can see on their scanner that a void continues, but they need to dig through collapsed rock to reach the next section.

**Void fill / rubble:** After block separation, loose material partially fills voids based on angle and width. Narrow gaps fill completely. Wide gaps get rubble floors but open ceilings. Vertical rifts stay mostly clear because rubble falls to the bottom. The rubble fill ratio is the primary connectivity tuning knob.

**Sector transitions** should be gradual blends over 20-30% of chunk width. Hard boundaries feel artificial. Sectors change character with depth — a rift zone near surface is cold basalt columns; at depth it's active magma flows. Same sector identity, different expression along the thermal gradient.

### Pass 3 — Fracture (Crack It)

Stress the rock until it breaks. Residual stress from tectonic forces causes fracture networks:

- Joints form perpendicular to the direction of least compression.
- Dense rock fractures into tight, angular networks.
- Softer rock deforms more before breaking — fewer but wider cracks.
- The vector byte stores dominant stress direction, guiding fracture orientation.

Fractures are the connectivity skeleton. Every water-carved cave starts as a crack from this pass. Cross-chunk connectivity: fracture lines are seeded deterministically at chunk edges (derived from world seed + chunk coordinates). If chunk (3,7) generates a fracture exiting its east edge at y=200, chunk (4,7) must pick it up.

### Pass 4 — Hydrology (Carve With Water)

Run fluid through the fracture network and tectonic voids. With the 15-minute budget, this can run hundreds of iterative sweeps, each modifying terrain slightly. The feedback loops that create real cave geometry actually play out.

Water does two things simultaneously:

**Erosion (upstream, fast flow):** Water flowing over a cell checks mineral values. Soft rock (low mineral density) — water picks up material, cell erodes, water's mineral load increases. Hard rock — water can't bite in, flows past unchanged. The water has a carrying capacity proportional to flow velocity. Narrow passages = fast flow = more erosive power = passage widens = flow slows = deposition begins. This self-limiting feedback is why real caves have wide chambers with sediment floors connected by narrow scoured passages.

**Deposition (downstream, slow flow):** When water slows or pools, it deposits what it's carrying. Dissolved minerals precipitate. Eroded material settles. Deposition happens in still pools, wide voids where velocity drops, basin floors. Sediment fills voids from the bottom up in horizontal layers — gravity-sorted, heavier particles first, finer material on top.

**Differential erosion:** Water flowing over mixed-mineral rock erodes the soft matrix faster than hard inclusions. This leaves pillars, overhangs, undercut walls, natural arches — all emerging from one mechanism applied to geologically varied rock.

**Mineral transport:** Water picks up traces of whatever mineral species it passes through and deposits them elsewhere. A shale deposit's mineral profile reflects the entire upstream path. Sediment from mineral-rich zones is mineral-rich. The player reads sediment chemistry as clues about upstream geology.

**Sediment compaction to shale:** Overburden pressure compresses deposited sediment. Loose sediment (low mineral density, easy to dig) gradually increases toward mid-range values (compacted, cohesive, but softer than parent rock). Compacted shale becomes aquifer caps — impermeable layers trapping water-saturated rock below. The player finds a shale layer and knows water might be underneath.

**Water settling:** After the hydrology pass completes, water occupies wherever physics put it. Pools at low points. Frozen where temperature is low enough. Steam where temperature is high enough. The water byte records the final state.

**Pressurized pockets (ONI-style):** Water flows into a void during hydrology. Sediment washes in after. Sediment compacts into shale, seals the void. Water is now trapped under overburden pressure proportional to depth. These are generated naturally by the pass sequence — nobody designed them. Pressure is derivable at breach time from depth + sealed water volume + enclosure size.

**Percolation:** Water soaks through material proportional to porosity (inverse of compaction). Loose sediment is permeable. Dense shale barely transmits. Solid rock is impervious. Over game-time, water percolating through a shale cap slowly weakens it — lowering mineral values along its path. Sealed pockets are ticking. They may self-breach given enough time.

## Pre-existing Features at Worldgen

### Ancient Biology — The Planet Was Alive Once

Oil deposits and fossils exist at worldgen. The planet had a biosphere that died. This changes the narrative: Biomimetica isn't starting from scratch — it's attempting a resurrection.

**Oil pockets:** Small (dozen cells). Scattered. Formed by the same process as player-created coal — ancient plant matter accumulated, buried, compressed over geological time. No massive deposits because this was a pre-animal world with no continent-scale die-offs. Each pocket is a local basin where vegetation collected in standing water. Oil is miner fuel (see below).

**Fossils:** The ancient biosphere was pre-animal. Two signatures:
- **Stromatolites** — Layered mineral structures from microbial mats. Concentric rings, wavy layers, dome shapes in rock. Banded patterns that are unmistakably biological but nothing you'd call a creature.
- **Plant compressions** — Flat carbon-rich smears in shale. Branching patterns too regular for mineral veins, too degraded to identify as specific species.

Fossils are billions of years old. Too degraded to reconstruct into organisms. The player identifies them by anomalous mineral ratios — cell value signatures that read as "this was alive once" without resolving into anything specific. The chemistry is wrong for pure geology.

No new species need to be designed. The fossils are abstract traces. The player theorizes about what lived here. The game never confirms.

### Water Distribution

Water is scarce and trapped at worldgen. Never free-standing pools available without effort:
- **Ice deposits** in shallow zones (low temperature, high water cells, frozen at generation time).
- **Sealed aquifers** at mid-depth (high-water cells bounded by high-mineral shale walls).
- **Geothermal steam** near core boundary (water in vapor state from high temperature).

The player must always *transform* water before it's useful: melt ice, breach aquifers carefully, condense steam. The planet is dead for a reason.

## Subtile Mask System

Caves are the primary play space and must feel like real places. The generation system uses a subtile mask vocabulary to guarantee playable geometry while letting geology fill everything else.

### Three-Layer Masking

Each subtile defines three material zones:

1. **Sacred (white)** — Air. Always. No generation pass fills it. Guaranteed platforming space from the start. Includes floor topology: shelves (raised ground) and dips (lowered ground for water collection).
2. **Soft Cap (grey)** — Generation fills this normally but enforces a maximum mineral density ceiling. Appears solid early game. Becomes passable as the player upgrades tools. This is the Metroid-style gating — no colored doors, just rock hardness on a curve.
3. **Wild (black)** — No mask. Pure geology. Whatever the four passes created. Could be anything.

**Critical rule:** Within a tile's intended traversal path, material is sacred or soft cap. Never wild. Wild only exists in areas that are not part of the tile's functional design. A step between two corridors at different heights must be sacred or soft cap — wild there defeats the tile's purpose.

### Connection System — 12-Slot

Each subtile edge has three connection slots:

- **Left / Right edges:** High (y=40), Mid (y=100), Low (y=160)
- **Top / Bottom edges:** Left (x=40), Center (x=100), Right (x=160)

Total: 12 slots per tile, each independently active or inactive. A tile's connection signature is a 12-bit mask. Matching between adjacent tiles is a bitwise AND on shared edges.

This enables:
- Corridors at different heights within horizontal tiles (high corridor vs low corridor).
- Shafts that aren't centered — connecting top-left to bottom-right for diagonal vertical movement.
- E-shaped tiles with a spine on the left connecting T:L to B:L, with branches reaching right at H, M, and L.
- Complex tiles like the step-down that enters L:H, exits R:M, and drops a shaft at B:R.

### Tile Vocabulary

**Passages:**
- `—` Corridor (high, mid, or low variants)
- `I` Shaft (centered or offset using L/C/R on top/bottom edges)
- `↘` Descent (slopes from L:H to R:L — diagonal without a shaft)
- `∕` Offset Shaft (T:L to B:R — shaft drifts laterally as it drops)
- `⊳` Pinch (narrows through soft cap — widens with upgrades)

**Junctions:**
- `T` Drop (corridor with shaft down — L:M + R:M + B:C)
- `⊥` Chimney (corridor with shaft up — L:M + R:M + T:C)
- `Y` Split (one entry forks to two exits at different heights)
- `⌐` Step + Drop (high entry, mid exit, off-center shaft — uses B:R)
- `+` Cross (four-way hub)
- `L` Corner (90° turn)

**Chambers:**
- `☽` Kidney Open — Fat curved chamber with narrow throat entrance, open both ends. Smile-shaped with a large belly. Throat is a natural soft cap zone. The belly is big enough to park a tank, set up equipment, grow things. Connection points at mid and low on both sides.
- `☽` Kidney Closed — Same shape but one end sealed. The closed end curves back toward the entrance, creating a thin wall between tip and throat (scannable, diggable shortcut). Water collects in the belly dip. Natural cistern.
- `O` Cathedral (large room, all 12 connection points active)
- `U` Sump (dead-end basin, water collects, single connection up)
- `C` Alcove (open one side, shelter)

**Complex:**
- `E` Multi-Branch (vertical spine open top/bottom, three horizontal branches at H/M/L)
- `Ǝ` Mirror Branch (E flipped — branches go opposite direction)
- `=` Double Deck (two stacked corridors with soft cap separator — early game two routes, later one tall room)
- `⊞` Ladder Shaft (shaft with alternating ledges at different connection heights — platforming inside a vertical drop)

### Floor Topology

Subtile masks include floor height variation as a first-class feature:

- **Shelves** — Raised ground above water line. Natural staging areas. Equipment, planting, construction. Safe during flooding.
- **Dips** — Lowered ground that collects water passively. Any water entering the chamber flows to the lowest point. Sediment accumulates in dips over time. A dip with accumulated sediment is a ready-made growing bed when drained.

A single chamber with both features (shelf + dip) is the most useful room in the game. Dry workspace with integrated water supply. Forward base material.

### SVG Mask Format

Subtile masks are defined as SVG files. Resolution-independent bezier curves rasterize to whatever cell resolution the subtile needs. Three fill colors map to the three mask layers. Connection points are markers on edges.

Benefits:
- Designers work visually — draw organic cave shapes, adjust curves.
- BZ patterns, voronoi cracks, fault lines can be SVG filter operations composited with masks.
- Version control is clean (diffable text).
- The subtile library is a folder of SVGs.
- All tiles rotate and mirror trivially.

## Digging Mechanics

There are no locked doors. Only cost.

Every material in the game is diggable. The magma plug above the machine is technically diggable with a starting tool — it would just take an absurd amount of time. The cost curve is exponential, not a wall.

Tool upgrades shift the efficiency curve, not a threshold. At any upgrade level, some material is easy, some is slow, and the densest stuff is technically possible but painfully expensive. The player decides how much to spend. Two players with the same tools experience the same room differently based on resource reserves.

The soft cap in layer 2 isn't a gate. It's a suggested timeline. The designer places soft cap material knowing that at the intended upgrade level it's a reasonable dig. Underequipped players can still get through at disproportionate cost.

Alternatives to brute-force digging:
- Breach a pressurized pocket sideways to let water erode a path.
- Drop a water bubble on lava at the edge to create obsidian and slowly reclaim terrain.
- Deploy a miner tank for slow unattended excavation.
- Use controlled flooding to erode soft material.

## Origin — The Starting Nine Chunks

Biomimetica hit this planet hard. It impacted, punched through the crust into deep rock, and the magma it displaced flowed back in behind it and cooled. The column above the machine is a solid igneous plug — the densest, hardest material in the game. Higher mineral density than anything at that depth naturally. No crater. No surface evidence. The wound healed. The only way out is sideways and down.

### The Machine

Approximately 1/3 of a chunk. Entirely sealed above by the solidified magma plug. The machine's interior is not shown (black box — dock, transition screen, return to world). The machine's exterior is visible and changes as the player gains upgrades: water capacity shows as external tanks, wireless recharger shows as antenna arrays, etc. Upgrades are physical geometry in the world, not menu items.

The wireless recharger creates a radius of safety around the machine. The player crosses back into range and the tank tops up without docking. This radius defines comfortable operating range and expands with upgrades.

Between runs, the machine's exterior upgrades persist but degrade over geological time — damaged, corroded, partially fossilized. The next instance reads previous upgrades as evidence of prior attempts.

### Tutorial Through Geology

The impact fractured everything around the machine in a radial pattern. Over thousands of years of dormancy:

- Impact fractures softened and rounded from water seepage.
- Sediment washed in and settled flat.
- A small aquifer formed where water pooled in the lowest fracture point, kept liquid by the machine's residual heat.
- The immediate area is gentle: flat sediment floors, rounded walls, standing water, soft light from the machine.

The player drives out of the machine into this comfortable space. The pond is the first interaction — drive through it, the tank picks up water. No tutorial text. Just a pond in the path.

Beyond the light range: a crumbling wall. The boundary between impact geology and natural geology. Impact fractures on this side (weathered, gentle, predictable). Natural cave network on the other side (angular, tight, varied). The wall is thin enough to collapse with a nudge. The contrast teaches natural cave character by first showing the domesticated version.

### Nine-Chunk Layout

- **Center chunk:** The machine, buried in impact-fractured rock. Fractures partially filled with sediment. Some sealed into pressurized pockets — the impact's own plumbing created the first energy sources.
- **Four cardinal chunks:** Fracture zones extending outward. These connect to the natural tectonic cave network at their far edges — the transition from impact geology to planetary geology.
- **Four corner chunks:** Less disturbed. Normal geology with slight tilt toward center from impact. Quiet zones for first surface experiments.

## Set Pieces and Points of Interest

Set pieces are defined as constraint sets, not fixed grids. A set piece specifies: "void of at least this size at approximately this depth, walls above this mineral density, sealed water pocket within this distance." Generation runs normally, then the set piece system finds locations where constraints are nearly met and nudges values to satisfy them.

For structures with fixed geometry (ruins, the machine), the structure is placed and then remaining generation passes run over it. Water flows around it. Sediment buries parts. Mineral deposits form against walls. The planet claims it. It's part of the rock, not sitting in a clean room.

**POI density:** Rare enough that finding one is an event. Approximately one per 3-4 chunks horizontally, with depth variance. They carve their own cavities rather than spawning inside natural caves — a crashed pod half-buried in rock with a small impact void feels discovered; one sitting in an existing cave feels placed.

**Old player spawns as POIs:** Previous run's spawn area becomes ruins. Excavated tunnels, partially terraformed zones, remnant biology. Coal seams where forests were. Mineral-depleted soil where they over-farmed. Concentrated deposits where biology ran wild. Lost tank chassis half-embedded in shale. Lost bodies composted into nutrient-rich deposits. The fossil record is the narrative.

## Pressurized Pockets — Energy and Hazards

Hot pressurized water pockets are the planet's stored energy. Sealed naturally during generation when hydrology fills a void, sediment seals it, and overburden creates pressure proportional to depth.

### Two Extraction Modes

**Blow it open:** Pressurized water does in seconds what the generation pass simulated over millennia. Violent local erosion — water floods connected caves, scours soft rock, deposits sediment downstream, reshapes terrain. The player trades a stored energy source for free excavation. The pocket only exists once. Irreversible.

**Tap with infrastructure:** Controlled extraction. Sustained energy output that tapers as pressure drops. The player must find new pockets or dig deeper for hotter ones. Resource management curve from thermodynamics.

**Middle ground:** Controlled breach at an angle. Let it partially erode a desired path, then cap before the pocket fully drains. Risky — unexpected voids can cause rapid pressure loss. Skill-based resource management.

**Chain reactions:** Two sealed pockets near each other — breach one into the other for cascading results. Emergent, dangerous, shareable.

### Pocket Behavior

- Deep pockets = hot pressurized water = more energy but more dangerous.
- Superheated steam at core boundary depths. Breach = instant lethal zone. Seconds to escape.
- Pockets weaken over time from percolation. A safe pocket may self-breach later. Player-introduced water above a pocket (irrigation) percolates down and accelerates cap failure.
- Spent water from energy extraction is warm depressurized water — the exact input for terraforming. The power source and the terraforming resource are the same fluid at different stages.

## Lava Interaction

Water dropped into lava produces obsidian and steam.

**Center of lava pond:** Water flash-boils. A blob of obsidian forms at the contact point, sinks to the bottom as a dense gem. Recoverable only if the player later drains or cools the entire pond. Expensive mistakes that literally sink.

**Edge of lava pond:** Lava is thin at margins. Water cools the edge — a small obsidian crust forms, shoreline advances inward by a few cells. Repeatable. The player slowly reclaims land from lava one bubble at a time. Endgame patience project: building an obsidian causeway across a lava lake, bubble by bubble.

**Steam hazard:** Every water-lava interaction produces superheated steam. The pod takes continuous damage in steam. Drop and fly immediately. Hesitate and that's a body. The risk is always the steam. The reward is always the obsidian.

Obsidian is the hardest diggable material the player can produce. Tools, structural reinforcement. Only manufacturable where lava meets water — deep, dangerous, expensive.

## Fire Mechanics

Temperature and moisture interact to create fire behavior.

**Propagation rule:** Fire spreads when temperature exceeds moisture's cooling capacity in a cell. The fire raises temperature in adjacent cells, which may dry those cells below threshold, causing cascade.

**Healthy biome = self-extinguishing.** A well-terraformed area with active water cycling has enough moisture in every cell to resist ignition. Fires die in a few cells. If an area burns, the player wasn't maintaining the hydrology. Fire is a diagnostic.

**Lava exception:** Continuous heat source. Even healthy wet biomes can't compensate at the lava margin. Temperature input exceeds moisture buffering. Fire-resistant plants are required here — derived from studying fossil signatures near geothermal zones (the planet's original biology adapted to heat).

**Fire-resistant plants** serve as firebreaks. The player rings lava-adjacent operations with heat-tolerant species that don't dry out, don't ignite, don't propagate flame.

**Fire enriches soil.** Ash is concentrated minerals from everything plants pulled from the ground. Mineral values spike in burned cells. But fire strips nitrogen (volatilizes into atmosphere) while leaving phosphorus and potassium. Post-fire soil is strong-rooted and tough but slow-growing until nitrogen is biologically restored (nitrogen-fixing plants, mushroom cycling).

**Ecological cycles:** Growth → drought/neglect → fire → ash enrichment → regrowth from richer base. A player who manages controlled burns has the most productive land. A player who never lets anything burn has stable but slowly declining soil. Slash-and-burn agriculture, emergent from temperature and moisture mechanics.

**Cascading feedback:** Fire kills plants → dead plants stop transpiring → soil dries further → fire spreads easier. Conversely: healthy water cycle dampens adjacent dry zones → moisture spreads through root networks → wet patches buffer neighbors. Life protects life. Death accelerates death.

## Water Cycle and Terraforming

The player's primary job is hydrology. They are plumbers. Biology follows water automatically.

### Sediment = Soil

Water flowing across barren rock deposits sediment. Sediment on rock is soil. Biology colonizes soil without player intervention. A single water channel across a barren shelf leaves a trail of sediment. Plants colonize the trail. Roots spread sideways. The trail widens. Positive feedback loop.

### Floodplain Farming

Ponds accumulate mineral-rich sediment over time. Drain a pond and the exposed mud is the richest growing medium on the planet — concentrated nutrients from every cell the water passed through. First crop from drained pond mud is the best yield in the game. But it's a one-time withdrawal. Nutrients deplete. The player moves on, routes water to a new dip, lets it accumulate, drains when ready. Rotational pond farming — managing silt, not just water.

Old drained ponds are flat, soft, worked depressions. Perfect foundations for structures. Or re-flood and restart the accumulation cycle. Land cycles between pond, farm, and building site.

### Percolation to Surface

Water established underground percolates upward through soil, dampening the surface layer from below. The dead barren surface starts showing moisture. Soil forms. Biology takes hold. The player doesn't pipe water to the surface — they establish underground hydrology and the surface terraforms itself.

### Transplanting

The player can scoop an established ecosystem chunk — soil, roots, moisture, microbes — seal it in a bubble, and tow it via pod to a new location. The transplant arrives already established. No waiting for colonization from scratch.

The transplant carries the chemistry of its origin. Pond mud transplanted to a dry cave still has concentrated nutrients. The biology adapts to local conditions but starts from a rich base.

The bubble tow is the pod's most vulnerable state. Slower, less maneuverable, can't fit through tight passages. Drop the bubble and it breaks — soil scatters, plants die, the harvested patch has a hole. Successful transplants leapfrog biology across the map. Multiple isolated colonies growing simultaneously, eventually merging.

## Resource Loops

### Miner Tanks

The player can sacrifice a tank (when they can manufacture replacements) by dropping it at a location with the pod. The tank becomes an autonomous miner — it digs in a given direction, slowly, unattended. It leaves a loose tunnel half-full of semi-processed dirt and extracted minerals for easy harvest.

Miner effectiveness depends on the tech level at creation. Early tanks are crude — slow, wasteful, can't handle dense rock. Late-game tanks chew clean and process efficiently. Deployed tanks are not upgradeable — replaced with better ones.

**Fuel:** Miners run on oil or raw hydrocarbons. The player must supply fuel — fly the pod out with fuel, top up the tank, collect harvest, fly back. This creates supply routes and reasons to repeatedly traverse the cave network. Forgetting a deployed tank means it sits idle — a future fossil.

**Between runs:** Deployed tanks persist. Geological time buries and corrodes them. The tunnel they carved becomes part of the geology. The next instance finds a suspiciously straight tunnel with a fossilized tank chassis at one end.

### Oil and Fuel

Pod fuel is infinite at base. Miner fuel must be found or produced. Ancient oil deposits exist at worldgen (small pockets, finite, positional). Early game: scavenge fossil hydrocarbons. Mid game: plant forests and wait for biomass accumulation. Late game: player-produced biology generates renewable fuel. The arc is dependency on fossil resources transitioning to renewable biology.

### Biology Becomes Geology

Plants concentrate minerals from soil through roots (mineral values in soil cells decrease as plants grow). If the player grows a forest and abandons it, biomass accumulates. Nothing on this planet evolved cellulose decomposers yet (unless the player introduces them). Dead material stacks, gets buried, overburden compresses it, temperature processes it.

Given enough game-time, a forgotten forest becomes a coal seam. A planned crop of mineral-concentrating plants becomes an ore deposit when compressed. Every biological action is also a geological action on a longer timescale. The player is a geological force.

## Repair Mechanics

Two strategies for sealing weak spots (weakening shale caps, crumbling walls, breach risks):

**Putty (tank-fired):** Immediate, mechanical, finite. Fills cells, mineral value jumps. Manufactured from mined resources. Inert — doesn't grow, adapt, or respond. Holds until pressure exceeds its strength, then fails catastrophically. A patch, not a fix.

**Mushrooms:** Slow, biological, self-sustaining. Mycelium threads through porous material, binding it, increasing effective mineral density organically. Takes time to establish but self-healing — grows denser where flow is highest, fills new cracks. Requires water and nutrients to survive. May spread beyond intended area. Has second-order ecological consequences.

**Belt and suspenders:** Putty over mushrooms on critical seals.

## Between-Run Processing

When Biomimetica goes dormant, geological time passes before the next run. The planet processes everything the previous run left behind:

- Forests compress into coal seams.
- Mineral-concentrating plants become ore deposits.
- Mushroom networks petrify into fossil structures.
- Water systems keep percolating, reshaping underground.
- Shale caps weaken and may self-breach.
- Pressurized pockets redistribute.
- Lost bodies compost into nutrient deposits.
- Lost tanks corrode into mineral-rich salvage POIs.
- Radioactive forests remain thermally active.
- Depleted soil remains depleted.

The next Biomimetica instance wakes to a planet shaped by all prior runs. The previous run's biology is this run's geology. The fossil record is the story. No logs, no journals, no text. Geology is the narrative medium.

## Visual Identity

The nine mineral channels drive base hue before any biology exists. High iron shifts warm (oxides). High copper shifts teal/green. High silicon shifts crystalline blue. High sulfur shifts yellow-green. High radioactives shift with a distinctive glow. Temperature shifts toward incandescence at depth.

The geology itself has visual variety. The player is visually rewarded for exploration before introducing any life. Biology then transforms base palettes rather than replacing them. A cave's appearance after terraforming reflects both its geology and what grew there.

Stromatolite fossils share visual DNA with BZ-generated mineral patterns — concentric rings, spiral structures. Life and geology from the same algorithmic source.

## Constraints Summary

- Cell encoding is continuous. No discrete material types. Behavior and rendering from value ratios.
- 16 bytes per cell total across all layers. Nine mineral channels in the geology layer.
- CPU simulation, GPU rendering. Sequential moisture/mineral transfers require CPU.
- Chunks don't simulate until visited. Worldgen is a one-shot pass per chunk (but the pass can be heavyweight — up to 15 minutes for initial world generation).
- Deterministic from seed. Edge features derived from world seed + chunk coordinates for cross-chunk continuity.
- Three separate world saves for three difficulty levels. Each accumulates independently.
- Reference DNA: Noita (pixel sim), Dwarf Fortress (water pressure), Oxygen Not Included (pressurized pockets), Metroid (ability gating via soft cap), Solar Jetman (pod flight, body/tank loss), Scorched Earth (ballistic tools), Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction (mineral vein generation).

## Open Questions

- Exact BZ parameterization per mineral species — needs experimentation.
- Tectonic pass implementation — full stress simulation vs. convincing approximation using Voronoi regions with displacement.
- Hydrology iteration count vs. quality vs. generation time tradeoff.
- Scanner resolution and range — how much can the player see through rock?
- Exact soft cap density ceilings per upgrade tier (loosely linked, not thresholds).
- Creature system interactions with fire, water, and cave ecology (separate design doc).
- Narrative beats — what killed the original biosphere? The game may never answer this explicitly.
